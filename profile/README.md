## The Pathphysiology of Diabetic Retinopathy

Diabetic retinopathy (DR) is a highly specific microvascular complication of diabetes, characterized by progressive damage to the blood vessels of the retina, the light-sensitive tissue at the back of the eye.

This condition, driven by chronic hyperglycemia, is a leading cause of preventable blindness among working-age adults globally. The disease often develops without any noticeable symptoms in its early stages, with vision changes only occurring after significant and sometimes irreversible damage has occurred. 

The progression of DR follows a well-defined clinical pathway, typically advancing through several stages that form the basis for clinical management and, consequently, for the proposed predictive model's task. The disease is broadly categorized into two main types: 

1. Non-proliferative diabetic retinopathy (NPDR), and
2. proliferative diabetic retinopathy (PDR)


## Non-Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (NPDR)

Represents the earlier phase of the disease. It is characterized by a series of pathological changes within the existing retinal vasculature. As high blood sugar damages the small blood vessels, they begin to weaken and leak. This stage is further subdivided based on the severity and extent of these changes:

1. **Mild NPDR**: This is the earliest detectable stage, defined by the presence of microaneurysmsâ€”small, balloon-like swellings in the retinal capillaries. At this point, vision is typically unaffected, and the primary clinical action is monitoring and patient education on glycemic control.
   
2. **Moderate NPDR**: As the disease advances, the vascular damage becomes more pronounced. More blood vessels swell and may become blocked, impeding blood flow to parts of the retina. This stage may be associated with the appearance of other signs, such as dot-and-blot hemorrhages, hard exudates (lipid deposits from leaky vessels), and cotton wool spots, which indicate small areas of retinal ischemia.6 Patients may begin to experience blurry vision.
 
3. **Severe NPDR**: This stage represents a critical juncture. A significant number of blood vessels become blocked, leading to widespread retinal ischemia. The oxygen-deprived retina responds by releasing growth factors, such as Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor (VEGF), which send signals to the body to grow new blood vessels. This stage is a direct and high-risk precursor to the most advanced form of the disease.
